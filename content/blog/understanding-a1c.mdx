# Understanding Your A1C: What the Numbers Mean

If you live with diabetes — or have recently been diagnosed — your A1C (also called HbA1c or glycated hemoglobin) is one of the most important numbers in your care. But what does it actually tell you?

## What Is A1C?

A1C measures your average blood sugar level over the past 2–3 months. Unlike a finger-prick glucose test (which shows your blood sugar at a single moment), A1C gives your care team a longer-term picture of how well your blood sugar is being managed.

It's expressed as a percentage. The higher the percentage, the higher your average blood sugar has been.

## What Do the Numbers Mean?

| A1C Level | What It Suggests |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Below 5.7% | Normal range |
| 5.7% – 6.4% | Pre-diabetes |
| 6.5% or higher | Diabetes |

For most people with diabetes, Diabetes Canada recommends a target A1C of **7.0% or lower** to reduce the risk of complications. However, your specialist may set a different target based on your age, health history, risk of hypoglycemia, and other factors.

## How Often Should It Be Checked?

- **Every 3 months** if your treatment has recently changed or your A1C is above target
- **Every 6 months** if your diabetes is well-controlled and stable

## What Can Affect Your A1C?

Several factors beyond blood sugar can influence A1C results, including iron deficiency, kidney disease, certain hemoglobin variants, and recent blood transfusions. Your specialist will consider these when interpreting your results.

## What If My A1C Is Above Target?

An above-target A1C doesn't mean you've failed — it means there may be an opportunity to adjust your treatment. This could involve medication changes, insulin optimization, dietary adjustments, or addressing other factors like stress and sleep.
